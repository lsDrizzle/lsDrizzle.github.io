Perceptron

Model: f(x)=sign(wTx)f(x)=\text{sign}(w^Tx)f(x)=sign(wTx), where x,w∈Rpx, w\in \R^px,w∈Rp.

We assume that the samples are yi∈{0,1},i=1,...,Ny_i\in\{0, 1\}, i=1,..., Nyi​∈{0,1},i=1,...,N. Then the loss function of a perceptron can be written as

L(w)=∑xi∈D−yiwTxiL\left( w \right) =\sum_{x_i\in D}{-y_iw^Tx_i} L(w)=xi​∈D∑​−yi​wTxi​

where DDD is the set containing all the samples which are incorrectly classified.

Then we can simply use stochastic gradient descent (SGD) to train the perceptron. If the samples are linearly separable, the perceptron can correctly classify all the samples eventually.

Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA)

Suppose we have two types of samples and want to classify them (let us say ) using a hyperplane wTx=0w^Tx=0wTx=0. Its normal vector is www and the projection of the iiith sample xix_ixi​ on the vector is zi=wTxiz_i=w^Tx_izi​=wTxi​. The mean value of ziz_izi​s is zˉ=1N∑i=1NwTxi\bar{z}=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N{w^Tx_i}zˉ=N1​∑i=1N​wTxi​ and the variance of them is Sz=1N∑i=1N(zi−zˉ)2S_z=\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N{\left( z_i-\bar{z} \right)}^2Sz​=N1​∑i=1N​(zi​−zˉ)2.

There are two types of samples, therefore we define zˉ\bar{z}zˉ in the two types as zˉ1\bar{z}_1zˉ1​ and zˉ2\bar{z}_2zˉ2​, respectively. We define the variance in the same way.

We hope to find a suitable www in order to make the classification more effectively. In the view of LDA, we hope to maximize the following objective function:

J(w)=(zˉ1−zˉ2)2Sz1+Sz2J\left( w \right) =\frac{\left( \bar{z}_1-\bar{z}_2 \right) ^2}{S_{z1}+S_{z2}} J(w)=Sz1​+Sz2​(zˉ1​−zˉ2​)2​

In this case, the projections of the two types of samples on www can stay far from each other and the projections of an individual type can be more crowded.

The denominator of J(w)J(w)J(w) can be written as

Sz1+Sz2=wT[1N1∑i=1N(xi−xˉ1)(xi−xˉ1)T]w+Sz2=wTS1w+Sz2=wT(S1+S2)w=wTSww\begin{aligned} S_{z1}+S_{z2}=&w^T\left[ \frac{1}{N_1}\sum_{i=1}^N{\left( x_i-\bar{x}_1 \right) \left( x_i-\bar{x}_1 \right) ^T} \right] w+S_{z2} \\ =&w^TS_1w+S_{z2}=w^T\left( S_1+S_2 \right) w=w^TS_ww \end{aligned} Sz1​+Sz2​==​wT[N1​1​i=1∑N​(xi​−xˉ1​)(xi​−xˉ1​)T]w+Sz2​wTS1​w+Sz2​=wT(S1​+S2​)w=wTSw​w​

where S1S_1S1​ and S2S_2S2​ are the covariance matrices.

The numerator can be written as

(zˉ1−zˉ2)2=(wT(1N1∑i∈C1xi−1N2∑i∈C2xi))2=wT(xˉ1−xˉ2)(xˉ1−xˉ2)Tw=wTSbw\begin{aligned} \left( \bar{z}_1-\bar{z}_2 \right) ^2=&\left( w^T\left( \frac{1}{N_1}\sum_{i\in C_1}{x_i}-\frac{1}{N_2}\sum_{i\in C_2}{x_i} \right) \right) ^2 \\ =&w^T\left( \bar{x}_1-\bar{x}_2 \right) \left( \bar{x}_1-\bar{x}_2 \right) ^Tw=w^TS_bw \end{aligned} (zˉ1​−zˉ2​)2==​(wT(N1​1​i∈C1​∑​xi​−N2​1​i∈C2​∑​xi​))2wT(xˉ1​−xˉ2​)(xˉ1​−xˉ2​)Tw=wTSb​w​

We call SbS_bSb​ the between-class variance and SwS_wSw​ the within-class variance.

Then the partial derivative of J(w)J(w)J(w) is

∂J(w)∂w=2Sbw(wTSww)−1−wTSbw(wTSww)−2Sww\frac{\partial J\left( w \right)}{\partial w}=2S_bw\left( w^TS_ww \right) ^{-1}-w^TS_bw\left( w^TS_ww \right) ^{-2}S_ww ∂w∂J(w)​=2Sb​w(wTSw​w)−1−wTSb​w(wTSw​w)−2Sw​w

Let the partial derivative be equal to 0, we can obtain

w=wTSwwwTSbwSw−1Sbw∝Sw−1(xˉ1−xˉ2)(xˉ1−xˉ2)Tw\begin{aligned} w=&\frac{w^TS_ww}{w^TS_bw}S_{w}^{-1}S_bw \\ \propto& S_{w}^{-1}\left( \bar{x}_1-\bar{x}_2 \right) \left( \bar{x}_1-\bar{x}_2 \right) ^Tw \end{aligned} w=∝​wTSb​wwTSw​w​Sw−1​Sb​wSw−1​(xˉ1​−xˉ2​)(xˉ1​−xˉ2​)Tw​

We only pay attention to the direction of www. Since (xˉ1−xˉ2)Tw∈R\left( \bar{x}_1-\bar{x}_2 \right) ^Tw\in \R(xˉ1​−xˉ2​)Tw∈R, the directions of www and Sw−1(xˉ1−xˉ2)S_{w}^{-1}\left( \bar{x}_1-\bar{x}_2 \right)Sw−1​(xˉ1​−xˉ2​) are the same.

Gaussian Discriminant Analysis (GDA)

Data: {(xi,yi)}i=1N\{(x_i, y_i)\}_{i=1}^N{(xi​,yi​)}i=1N​, xi∈Rpx_i\in\R^pxi​∈Rp, yi∈{0,1}y_i\in\{0, 1\}yi​∈{0,1}

We assume yyy yields to Bernoulli Distribution, that is, P(y=1)=ϕP(y=1)=\phiP(y=1)=ϕ and P(y=0)=1−ϕP(y=0)=1-\phiP(y=0)=1−ϕ. Additionally, we assume x∣y=1x|y=1x∣y=1 ~ N(μ1,Σ)N(\mu_1,\varSigma)N(μ1​,Σ) and x∣y=0x|y=0x∣y=0 ~ N(μ2,Σ)N(\mu_2,\varSigma)N(μ2​,Σ).

The GDA’s goal is to maximize the following function:

L(θ)=log⁡∏i=1N(P(xi∣yi)P(yi))=∑i=1N[log⁡P(xi∣yi)+log⁡P(yi)]=∑i=1N[log⁡N(μ1,Σ)yi+log⁡N(μ2,Σ)1−yi+log⁡ϕyi(1−ϕ)1−yi]\begin{aligned} L\left( \theta \right) =&\log \prod_{i=1}^N{\left( P\left( x_i|y_i \right) P\left( y_i \right) \right)}=\sum_{i=1}^N{\left[ \log P\left( x_i|y_i \right) +\log P\left( y_i \right) \right]} \\ =&\sum_{i=1}^N{\left[ \log N\left( \mu _1, \varSigma \right) ^{y_i}+\log N\left( \mu _2, \varSigma \right) ^{1-y_i}+\log \phi ^{y_i}\left( 1-\phi \right) ^{1-y_i} \right]} \end{aligned} L(θ)==​logi=1∏N​(P(xi​∣yi​)P(yi​))=i=1∑N​[logP(xi​∣yi​)+logP(yi​)]i=1∑N​[logN(μ1​,Σ)yi​+logN(μ2​,Σ)1−yi​+logϕyi​(1−ϕ)1−yi​]​

where θ=(μ1,μ2,Σ,ϕ)\theta=(\mu_1, \mu_2, \varSigma, \phi)θ=(μ1​,μ2​,Σ,ϕ).

Through partial derivatives, it is easy to obtain ϕ^=N1/N\hat\phi=N_1/Nϕ^​=N1​/N, where N1N_1N1​ is the number of yiy_iyi​s that equal to 1. And μ^1=∑i=1NyixiN1\hat\mu_1=\frac{\sum_{i=1}^N{y_ix_i}}{N_1}μ^​1​=N1​∑i=1N​yi​xi​​.

Now let’s find the optimum Σ\varSigmaΣ:

Σ^=argmax⁡Σ  ∑i=1N[yilog⁡N(μ1,Σ)+(1−yi)log⁡N(μ2,Σ)]=argmax⁡Σ  ∑i=1N1log⁡N(μ1,Σ)+∑i=1N2log⁡N(μ2,Σ)\begin{aligned} \hat{\varSigma}=&\underset{\varSigma}{\text{arg}\max}\,\,\sum_{i=1}^N{\left[ y_i\log N\left( \mu _1, \varSigma \right) +\left( 1-y_i \right) \log N\left( \mu _2, \varSigma \right) \right]} \\ =&\underset{\varSigma}{\text{arg}\max}\,\,\sum_{i=1}^{N_1}{\log N\left( \mu _1, \varSigma \right)}+\sum_{i=1}^{N_2}{\log N\left( \mu _2, \varSigma \right)} \end{aligned} Σ^==​Σargmax​i=1∑N​[yi​logN(μ1​,Σ)+(1−yi​)logN(μ2​,Σ)]Σargmax​i=1∑N1​​logN(μ1​,Σ)+i=1∑N2​​logN(μ2​,Σ)​

where N2N_2N2​ are the number of yiy_iyi​s that equal to 0.
