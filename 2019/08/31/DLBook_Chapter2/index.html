Singular Value Decomposition

Singular Value Decomposition (SVD): Suppose that AAA is an m×nm\times nm×n matrix, then its SVD can be written as

A=UDVTA=UDV^T A=UDVT

where UUU is an m×mm\times mm×m orthogonal matrix, DDD an m×nm\times nm×n orthogonal matrix and VVV an n×nn\times nn×n diagonal matrix. The elements along the diagonal of DDD are known as the singular values of AAA. The columns of UUU are known as the left-singular vectors. The columns of VVV are known as the right-singular vectors.

Every real matrix has an SVD.

The left-singular vectors of AAA are the eigenvectors of AATAA^TAAT. The right-singular vectors of AAA are the eigenvectors of ATAA^TAATA. The nonzero singular values of AAA are the square roots of the eigenvalues of ATAA^TAATA and AATAA^TAAT.

Moore-Penrose Pseudoinverse

Consider a linear equation Ax=yAx=yAx=y. Suppose we want to make a left-inverse BBB of AAA to solve the equation, i.e., we hope to obtain x=Byx=Byx=By. If AAA is taller than it is wide, it is possible for Ax=yAx=yAx=y to have no solution. If AAA is wider than it is tall, there could be multiple possible solutions.

We compute the pseudoinverse of AAA through the formula

A+=VD+UTA^+=VD^+U^T A+=VD+UT

where UUU, DDD and VVV are the singular value decomposition of AAA, and the pseudoinverse D+D^+D+ of the diagonal matrix DDD is obtained by taking the reciprocal of its nonzero elements then taking the transpose of the resulting matrix.

When AAA has more columns than rows, then x=A+yx=A^+yx=A+y is one of the solutions of Ax=yAx=yAx=y that has minimal Euclidean norm ∣∣x∣∣2||x||_2∣∣x∣∣2​. When AAA has more rows than columns, it is possible that there is no solution. In this case, x=A+yx=A^+yx=A+y makes AxAxAx as close as possible to yyy in terms of Euclidean norm ∣∣Ax−y∣∣2||Ax-y||_2∣∣Ax−y∣∣2​.

Trace Operator

Trace Operator: The trace operator gives the sum of all the diagonal entries of a matrix: Tr(A)=∑iAi,i\text{Tr}\left( A \right) =\sum_i{A_{i,i}}Tr(A)=∑i​Ai,i​.

The trace of a square matrix composed of many factors is also invariant to moving the last factor into the ﬁrst position, if the shapes of the corresponding matrices allow the resulting product to be deﬁned:

Tr(A1A2⋅⋅⋅An)=Tr(AnA1⋅⋅⋅An−1)\text{Tr}(A_1A_2···A_n)=\text{Tr}(A_nA_1···A_{n-1}) Tr(A1​A2​⋅⋅⋅An​)=Tr(An​A1​⋅⋅⋅An−1​)

This invariance to cyclic permutation holds even if the resulting product has a diﬀerent shape. For example, for A∈Rm×nA\in \mathbf{R}^{m\times n}A∈Rm×n and B∈Rn×mB\in \mathbf{R}^{n\times m}B∈Rn×m, we have Tr(AB)=Tr(BA)\text{Tr}(AB)=\text{Tr}(BA)Tr(AB)=Tr(BA).
